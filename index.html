Lore

" Most would say that what sparked the little boy's interest was his deeply-rooted love for video games. "

Born in La Habana, young Aldo spent most of his time playing with his childhood friends or studying. Whenever he felt he was not at the level he wanted to be at in a specific subject, he spent his nights studying. For mathematics, he would do the multiplication tables over and over again, all the way to triple-digits, before even reaching the 2nd grade. His parents, vowed to provide a good future for their son and older daughter—Cuba, with all its poverty and scarcity, would only stifle Aldo, whom they felt deserved the wealth and opportunities of La Yuma (The United States). His parents would work hard beyond their wage, to ensure that their son could at least enjoy his childhood.

It would all be worth it, they reasoned, if it meant their son could one day rise to the country above.

Aldo saw things differently. Beyond Cuba's flaws, he saw a dynamic place overflowing with energy and potential. Cubans' industry, resourcefulness, and resilience stirred a hotbed of pure innovation. They had built a thriving culture from catastrophe, and flourished where others would have perished. That spirit captivated Aldo, and spurred him to a youth of wild invention and innovation.

He wasn’t alone. He befriended neighborhood kids, inquisitive and eager youths. Cubans tended to eschew formal education in favor of apprenticeships, but these children of Cuba looked to the ruined streets to be their mentor. They wasted time in glorious, youthful fashion—foot races through the neighborhoods, or daring climbs from the ground to the tallest tree branch. They ran wild and free, answering to no one.

On his 5th birthday, Aldo's parents presented him with an astonishing gift: a Nintendo Entertainment System. Every child of Cuba heard tales of this technology, but not many could say they had seen one in person. So the first thing he did was invite all of his friends over. That first day they played for what seemed to be hours upon hours. This, of course, became a weekly tradition.

The children experimented madly with the console. During one intense session of Contra, the controller stopped working. Aldo tried reconnecting the controller, restarting the system, powering off the TV and back on again. Nothing. The room grew quiet. The deafening silence drew the boy's mother to the room and she could see in their eyes what they could not vocalize. Aldo felt as if he had just lost a childhood friend.

This was a big deal. The simple act of purchasing a video game console is seldom heard of in Cuba. But the young boy's mother spoke, and she insisted that they wait for his father to arrive from work; implying that he may be able to take a closer look at it.

There was hope.

Aldo's father arrived and was informed of the situation. Immediately, he extracted his tools from a tiny shed underneath the staircase and reached for a screwdriver and soldering iron. He proceeded to dissect the controller, exposing its internal circuitry. The boys stared in awe at the magic they were beholding. It was as if watching tiny metals melt into a silver glue. And just as quick as it started, it was done. His father put the controller back together and asked the children to try it.

It worked!

That day marked a pivotal point in the young man's life. Seeing such an impossible feat being conquered, systemmatically, created a tsunami of excitement in the little boy's heart. His quest for knowledge had only begun. Most would say that what sparked the little boy's interest was his deeply-rooted love for video games.
